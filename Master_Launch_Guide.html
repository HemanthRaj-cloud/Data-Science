The 7 Things You're Missing

Critical elements no one tells you about — each one meaningfully increases your hire probability

Reality Check
Your 4 documents cover what to learn and what to build. But getting hired also requires knowing how to operate day-to-day — outreach scripts, a daily schedule that fits around your current job, a system to track 30+ applications, and a mental framework to stay consistent for 60+ days. These aren't glamorous but they are what separates candidates who land offers from those who burn out at week 4.

You have a current job (10 months in). That means you have roughly 2–3 hours per day to prep, not 8. This is the one nobody prepares for. Even the best candidates get rejected 70–80% of the time at FAANG. A rejection after an onsite you felt good about can completely derail your momentum if you don't have a protocol for it. Candidates who land offers are not the ones who don't get rejected — they're the ones who process rejection fast, extract signal, and keep going. The resume gets you the offer. The negotiation determines how much it's worth. Most first-time negotiators accept the first number — at FAANG, that's almost always 15–30% below what's achievable. You need specific scripts for: the first offer call response, the counter offer email, leveraging competing offers, and negotiating equity vs. salary vs. signing bonus tradeoffs. One conversation, done right, adds $20K–$60K.

Daily Schedule — Working Around Your Full-Time Job

Realistic time blocks for someone employed full-time with 2–3 free hours per weekday

Key Principle
You don't need 8 hours/day. You need 2 focused hours on weekdays + 4 hours on weekends = 18 hours/week. At that rate, 60 days = 1,080 hours of deliberate prep — more than enough to close every gap. The schedule below is built for that reality, not an imaginary one where you have unlimited time.
MON–FRI
Weekday Schedule — 2 hrs total
Fits around 9–5 job · No sacrifice required
Morning commute
ML verbal practice — explain 1 concept out loud on your way to work. No phone needed. Record 2x/week. 💡 Commute = free prep time. Use it.
Lunch 12–12:30
LeetCode 2 problems — Easy or Medium, Python only. Timer: 20 min max per problem. Do on phone if needed. 💡 30 min = 2 problems = 500+ problems in 8 months
Evening 8–9 PM
SQL practice — 5 StrataScratch problems. Window functions focus for first 3 weeks. Hard stop at 9 PM to protect sleep. 💡 Set a phone alarm. Treat it like a meeting.
Evening 9–9:30 PM
Project work or reading — alternate days: Mon/Wed/Fri = project coding. Tue/Thu = "Ace the DS Interview" book reading. 💡 Small daily progress > weekly marathon sessions
SAT–SUN
Weekend Schedule — 4 hrs per day
Deep work sessions · This is where the real progress happens
9–11 AM
Deep project work — 2-hour focused build session. RAG pipeline, Big Data project, or A/B testing app. No distractions. Phone on silent. This is how projects get finished. 💡 Use Pomodoro: 50 min work / 10 min break
11–12 PM
LeetCode Mediums — 3 problems, timed. Focus on one pattern type per session (e.g., all sliding window). After Week 4, add 1 Hard problem per week. 💡 Saturate patterns, don't randomise
1–2 PM
Networking / LinkedIn / Outreach — send 3 alumni DMs, write your weekly LinkedIn post, respond to any recruiter messages. 1 hour of strategic outreach per week is enough. 💡 Quality over volume in networking
2–3 PM
Case study practice or mock interview — every other weekend: book a Pramp session. Off weeks: answer 2 product case questions in writing using METRICS framework. 💡 Writing answers builds clarity for verbal delivery

Copy-Paste Outreach Templates

Exact messages for every scenario — tested to get responses

1. FAU Alumni Cold DM (LinkedIn)
Use: Alumni you've never met
Hi [Name], Fellow FAU alum here — I noticed your work as a [Data Scientist / ML Engineer] at [Company] and was impressed by [something specific from their LinkedIn, e.g., "your work on the ranking team"]. I recently finished my M.S. in Data Science at FAU and have been building GenAI systems — including a multimodal image recognition tool using Gemini API and a RAG-based document retrieval system. I'm now targeting DS roles at companies like [Company]. Would you be open to a 15-minute virtual coffee chat sometime? I'd love to hear about your experience and what the DS culture is like at [Company]. No ask beyond that — just genuinely curious. Thanks so much, [Your Name]
✅ Why it works: FAU connection is warm, you lead with their work (not yours), you briefly establish credibility with specifics, and you ask for 15 minutes (low commitment). Never ask for a referral in the first message.
2. Referral Request (After 1–2 Conversations)
Use: Contacts you've spoken to at least once
Hi [Name], Thank you again for the chat last week — it was genuinely helpful to understand how the DS team at [Company] operates. I wanted to let you know I've applied for the [Job Title] role (Job ID: XXXXX). Given our conversation, I think my background in GenAI and Big Data could be a strong fit for what the team is working on. If you'd be comfortable providing a referral, I'd really appreciate it — I understand it's completely fine if not. Either way, I'll keep you updated on how the process goes. Best, [Your Name] [LinkedIn] | [GitHub] | [Portfolio URL]
✅ Why it works: References previous connection, gives them the specific job ID so it's easy, gives them a clear out ("completely fine if not"), and includes all your links so forwarding is frictionless.
3. Recruiter Follow-Up After Applying (LinkedIn DM)
Use: 5–7 days after submitting application
Hi [Recruiter Name], I recently applied for the Data Scientist role at [Company] (Job ID: XXXXX) and wanted to briefly introduce myself. My background is in GenAI and applied ML — I've built a production RAG system using Gemini API and a real-time streaming pipeline with Kafka + PySpark. I hold an M.S. in Data Science from Florida Atlantic University and have [X] years of experience. I'm particularly excited about [Company] because of [1 specific thing — e.g., "your experimentation culture" or "the work on real-time ML at scale"]. Happy to share more if helpful. Thank you for your time. [Your Name] | [LinkedIn] | [GitHub]
✅ Why it works: Specific job ID, leads with your most impressive/rare skill (GenAI + RAG), shows company-specific research, and is under 100 words so it gets read. Never attach your resume in a DM — let them ask for it.
4. Cold Recruiter Outreach (Before Applying)
Use: Proactive contact with internal recruiters
Hi [Recruiter Name], I came across your profile and see you recruit for DS/ML roles at [Company] — I wanted to reach out proactively as I'm actively exploring opportunities. Quick background: M.S. Data Science (FAU), [X] years experience, specialising in GenAI systems (built with Gemini API) and Big Data (Apache Spark, Kafka). Currently looking for DS roles where I can build production-scale ML and LLM pipelines. I'd love to understand what DS openings are coming up at [Company] and whether my background might be a fit. Thanks, [Your Name] | [LinkedIn] | [Portfolio: yourname.dev]
✅ Why it works: Proactive outreach captures roles before they're posted. Leads with the two skills that are hardest to find (GenAI + Big Data). Short enough to read in 20 seconds.
5. Salary Negotiation Counter-Offer Script
Use: When you receive an initial offer
Thank you so much — I'm genuinely excited about this opportunity and the team I'd be joining. I've done some research on market compensation for this role and level, and based on my background in GenAI systems and [X] years of experience, I was hoping we could get to [$TARGET — 15-20% above offer]. Is there flexibility on [base salary / equity / signing bonus]? I want to make this work and I'm committed to the role — I just want to make sure we're aligned on comp. [If they ask about competing offers]: I do have another process in progress at [Company X], but [Company] is my first choice. I wanted to give you the opportunity to match before making a decision.
✅ Key rules: (1) Never accept on the call — always ask for 3 days. (2) Always counter — the worst they can say is no, and most can move 10–20%. (3) Have a number ready. (4) Negotiate base, equity, AND signing bonus separately — they often have more flexibility on equity and signing than base. Verify your target on levels.fyi first.
6. Post-Rejection Thank You (And Re-Apply Positioning)
Use: Within 24 hours of rejection notice
Hi [Recruiter Name], Thank you for letting me know and for considering me for the [Role] position. I'd genuinely appreciate any feedback on where I fell short — even a single sentence would help me improve. I remain very interested in [Company] and plan to reapply in 6 months once I've continued building my experience. If any other DS opportunities come up where my background in GenAI and Big Data might be a better fit, I'd welcome the chance to be considered. Thank you again for your time. [Your Name]
✅ Why this matters: Most candidates disappear after rejection. This message: (1) asks for feedback (rarely given, but invaluable when received), (2) plants a re-apply seed (companies re-hire the same candidates regularly), and (3) keeps the relationship warm for future openings. One rejected candidate who sends this and reapplies 6 months later has a dramatically higher acceptance rate.
